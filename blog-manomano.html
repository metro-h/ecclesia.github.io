Growing the Church: Reflections From AOG BTG IN Eswatini

Pst S Manomano, Eswatini October 2023
Comments 40K Views

Introduction

Drawing from in–depth conversations with one of the senior Pastors in the Assemblies of God BTG Eswatini, Rev Hezekiah Nkambule, Eswatini work grew as a result of the late Rev Nicholas Bhekinkosi Hepworth Bhengu from the Republic of South Africa (RSA), as well as complementary team work involving Dr Elijah Maswangani, Mrs Hlapo and others from RSA. Beginning with crusades in October 1973, the work started massively and quickly spread to all provinces across the kingdom. Eventually, the church raised gospel ministers such as Dr. Douglas Simelane, and many others who joined, like Pastor Lukhele. Together, they produced many other ministers such as Pastor Mabuza who moved to Namibia as a missionary.

Structure of the work and leadership style

The Eswatini AOG BTG operates as a region of AOG (BTG) Southern Africa under a seven membered ‘National’ General Executive team that leads South Africa, Eswatini Lesotho, Namibia, Botswana and Mozambique.

Unique features of the work: Leadership style

The church leadership style is consultative and office bearers are so humble. Their humility is the hallmark of the church’s growth. Leaders respect senior pastors through various ways such as tasking them to introduce speakers at regional, national events as well as closing events with prayers. The profiling of ministry is something that one cannot fail to notice. Nobody goes to the stage unless they have specific tasks to perform. That way, one can hardly notice who the leaders, i.e. chairman of the Regional council is or even who the members of the team. Currently, Pastor Mamba is the regional chairman. He sometimes leads by absence. When anyone thinks he will go up to say something he delegates or is strategically not at that point. Such aspects seem to advance missionary servant-hood!

Values

There is deep godly values embedded in the work. Sin is treated as such with expressive love for the sinner. Those who flout agreed standards still get public rebuke.Other notable features include respect for the long established emphasis on prayer. The church conservatively holds fasting and prayers on Mondays, worship and main service on Sundays. Someone once remarked that if you need to ‘experience’ the Assemblies of God the place to be is Eswatini.

Evangelism

Evangelism and witnessing have been a unique feature and growth factor. This continues to be celebrated in all major gatherings with the BTG tent team giving reports and the new converts being introduced to the church at large. As John Maxwell writes in one of the books about the things that are monitored, the things that are reported and monitored get done. The church developed a template for men's quarterlies with some interesting features to monitor evangelism and soulwinning efforts in the men's work. The template has a column for numbers of saved men, preaching points, spiritual growth and miracles experienced. As such, the church has a huge following attracting people from all walks of life. It has branches throughout all the four regions of the country. It owns a farm on which a national centre will be erected. Additionally, the church boasts of many church buildings as well as church sites being developed.

Groups

It is reported that from the very onset, groups played a key feature in the growth of the work. ‘He directed the disciples to sit all the people in groups as though they were having a banquet on the grass. They sat down in groups of hundreds and fifties’ Mark 6:39-40 CEB. In the miracle of the feeding of the multitudes, there is the emphasis that the people sat down in groups. The work functions with special emphasis on the groups namely men, women, youth (Boys and girls), Girls, and Sunday school

Group leadership

Each group is led by organizers. These are recognized church officers who run the groups throughout the work. They carry sufficient respect and authority for the effective achievement of their given goals for the group. ‘This happened even though he was one of us and received a share of this ministry’ Acts 1:17. The organizers take their work seriously and diligently. They elect committees for their specific groups and visit regularly to monitor the work. Something worth noting is the depth of the leaders both spiritually and the level of understanding of their group needs. They teach and preach often in the gatherings of their respect groups with such anointing that every encounter with them is an experience. One recent case in point is the Men Quarterly in the Manzini East Region where Organizer Jerry Ndlovu was teaching on the subject ‘How to prepare for retirement and live after retirement’. Organizer Ndlovu also shared the word of God on the subject of prayer. Another outstanding leader of the group is Mrs Simalane. She recently was invited to teach on the women’s ministry in the new BTG work in Zambia. There are similar cases for youth and Sunday school work. Youth organizer Sandile Makanya is also another amazing bundle of creativity and commitment. These few names feature as those whose international appeal beyond Eswatini is becoming very evident

Areas that need attention

This glorious work still has areas they need to improve on in so far as the Cape to Cairo mantra is concerned.

English language as a tool

An interesting story is told of a teenager in one of the assemblies. He struggled to testify and recite scripture in the Siswati language simply because he had memorized it in English. He then revealed that his grandmother had told him it was not being respectful speaking in the English language. Gogo’s philosophy had affected his thinking. One hopes that the work gets to adopt a radical approach towards this ‘gogo’s spell. This manifests in the following areas:- a)Youth/Girls do invitations for their meetings in the vernacular b)Reports for monthlies, quarterlies, and conventions for most regions are presented in the vernacular. c)Preaching at Conventions including youth needs interpretation to allow a wider audience. Improvements in the acceptance of English will make the work more inclusive and friendly. This is also for the children of parents in church. Currently, the work is quite unfriendly and it is hoped that in due time the leadership will give guidance on this.

Conclusion

One could safely say that the international brand that the Assemblies is has sadly been too localised. There is need to raise many leaders in the mould of the late I.D Hleta, Pastor Hlophe, Pastor Lukhele, Pastor Mabuza who saw the work beyond the kingdom of Eswatini. One recommendation would be to send Pastors for Bible school training in other countries. That will help to produce leaders who will think globally and act locally. Globalisation is an urgent need of the work.
