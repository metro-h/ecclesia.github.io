Sustaining Pastoral Ministry - Rethinking Tent Making

Pastor Daniel Chiweda, Harare Zimbabwe January 2022
Comments 25K Views
Introduction

(Mathew 25:15, “And unto one he gave five talents, to another two, and to another one; to every man according to his several ability; and straightway took his journey”). It is remarkable to note that God gave everyone talents according to their ability. As such, nobody can stand and accuse Him of not having received anything. Pastors are not excluded in this universal plan of God. Starting right from your calling, God gave each one something and made out callings different according to our capabilities.

The calling for everyone therefore, is to apply the talents as provided by the master. It is discernible that everyone was born to solve a certain problem. The size of the problem you’re solving determines the seat you're allocated in this journey of life. Your remuneration is directly proportional to the impact of your solution to the equation of life. All people – Christians and non– Christian, are inherently selfish. They decide your worth, purely based on how much they can benefit from your service. A Pastor, therefore first and foremost, must be careful of the purpose of the pulpit which they have been given as their first tent.

The Pastor therefore has two important tasks;

  • To lead the people in worshiping God
  • To make the God of the Bible become an absolute reality that impacts the life of believers. How can you threaten people of hell, if they are already living in hell on earth? Thus the pulpit, must transform the ordinary lives of people to experience heaven on earth. That is the first tent of the ministry. Use your pulpit to solve the problems of the people, heal the sick, fix broken marriages, restore father and sons and mentor the youth.

    • How can you measure the impact of your pulpit?

  • How many people in your congregation consult you before making life changing decisions?

  • Do your congregants refer others who have problems to come to you for help?

  • How many of your congregants associate their success to your pulpit?

  • What’s the impact of your church to the community in which you operate?

  • What’s the impact of your church to the community in which you operate?

  • Are you available for weddings, funerals, graduation, birthday celebrations of congregants?

    • Your congregation is not mostly interested in the correctness of your theology than its impact on their lives. Those who want to produce theologians out of their congregation, will soon find themselves with very few students, whose visit to the church is to inquire on the interpretation of last week’s sermon, instead of worshiping of God, or finding solutions to their problems. This means the Pastor has a responsibility to increase his skill–set according to the ability given by the Maker. It is also crucial to ask questions such as; how many books a pastor read? How wide is his/her network, both with other pastors and with the world? Money is with the people according to Luke 6:38, “man shall add to your bosom”. Notice that it doesn't say God will give you money. The money is with the people. In order to increase streams of income, a pastor has the responsibility to take advantage of the talents at their disposal. Many are called into ministry after having attained a certain levels of education. But all this is quickly thrown out of the windows in pursuit of the calling of God

    And who could falter such a noble desire? But in as much Paul was a tent maker, Peter was a fisherman. Peter in John 21, tried to go back to fishing and the result was a lot of questions from the Lord. Peter do you love me, three times and the admonishment. Feed my flocks. Paul on the other hand made tents during the night and preached the gospel during the day. Some of us will have to tend the sheep fulltime and some will have to make tents. The important thing is that he who gave talents, gave according to our abilities. And the same God who decided to give these abilities is the same who gave the talents. The challenge of pastors is therefore is aligning the talents with their abilities. If you are educated, take advantage of that talent and use it as a source of solving problems. The challenge has always been that when we think of tent–making, we think of a vegetable garden or a fowl run. It is God who gave you that skill set, and there’s no shame in the doctor seeing patients during the day and preaching at the crusade at night. Whatever you are doing wherever you are, your streams of income are directly proportional to the problems you are solving. And who has the biggest problem to solve, if it is not the pastor, who solves the world’s most difficult problem.


    Conclusion

    The human race cannot be locked up by the most sophisticated computer program, neither can the soul of man be exposed by the biggest and fastest microscope. Yet only the pastor through the grace of our Lord Jesus Christ, tame that soul and bring it to its knees. The depravity of the human soul is still the greatest problem of the world. The Lord Jesus said, “the labourer is worth his hire”
