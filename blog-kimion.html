Loosing an Argument and Gaining Peace

Dr Kimion Tagwirei, Bulawayo Zimbabwe April 2022
Comments 41K Views

Introduction

Recently, before the Zimbabwean president revoked a government ban on private urban transport operators on Saturday, 7 May 2022 due to worsening transport crisis after the majority of those who had hired their vehicles to ZUPCO had withdrawn their buses in protest against poor payments and management, I boarded an unlicensed commuter omnibus from home to town and unknowingly triggered an argument that turned hotly provocative. Seating in the front, I realized that the driver was my neighbor, and the owner of the commuter omnibus. I jovially greeted him and asked how he was faring. In a loud voice, he said, “tight, it’s survival of the fittest – spiritually, politically and physically! They tried to force all of us to join ZUPCO, but that’s not what some of us wanted. I wanted to run my business my own way. I also hate monopolization of transport business, it’s like facing the anti-Christ who seeks to control everyone and all things. I would continue operating as mshikashika (privately and illegally) and never submit to ZUPCO. ”

I sympathized with him and said that I pray for our representatives to continue engaging the government to address such issues as part of being open to business. A fellow passenger commented that the incumbent political leaders do not respect peaceful engagements, “the only way they understand is violence, and by the way, even the Bible says that the kingdom of God suffers violence and the violent shall take it by force” (referring to Mathew 11:12). I responded that sometimes we take Bible verses out of context, misunderstand and misfire, but my humble view is that, if the Zimbabwean leaders are violent as he perceived, citizens may not win peace and progress by using the same strategy. I submitted that violence cannot curb violence, and progress cannot be realized by retaliation. Charged, frowning his face in antagonism and visibly shaking like a reed in the middle of a flooded river, the man raised his head highly, opened his eyes fully, and, with a booming voice, roared that Zimbabwe will never be fixed by peaceful engagements and ballot boxes.

Subsequently, warring voices vied for space and overwhelmed the bus as others opposed and some supported him. A conflict had erupted and dramatically grown tense, threatening to get vicious! I humbly agreed to disagree, and politely withdrew from the issue. Shortly, everyone dropped off in town. The man of the violent perspective walked out as the winner – we lost the argument, but behold, won peace! In a similarly hot case, two commercial sex workers once had a fatal quarrel at home, as recounted in the Bible by 1 Kings 3:16–27. One slept over, killed her baby and took her companion’s child. Waking up with a dead child besides her, the real mother argued that hers was alive, and the fellow claimed the same. They eventually engaged the wise king Solomon and he judged that the living child could be cut into two to share them equally. One shouted, “Yes, kill him and share us!”, and the rightful mother chose to lose the argument and win peace, and life for her baby. She pleaded with the king not to kill the child, but rather give her fellow! The King discerned that she was the actual mother and gave her the baby alive. Such is an enlightening example of losing a dispute to win peace, and life. When someone, or some people turn ferocious over a conflict, it is better to lose the argument as that begets peace and life.

Conclusion

A rigid position to always win arguments sometimes attracts violence when involved parties take the same stance. As we approach elections in Zimbabwe, we may face several conflicting standpoints in different areas. It is wise to lose a turbulent perception in order to gain peace, and life. Even when we feel that we are right, at times withdrawing from certain conflicts is best to realize peace. Sooner or later, the king of kings – God will judge and vindicate us! The same applies to all encounters of life – retreating and losing some arguments is wise for us to gain peace and life in stormily conflictive circumstances.
