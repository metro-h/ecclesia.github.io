Overcoming Addiction

Boanergegs Davesfield, Harare Zimbabwe April 2022
Comments 31K Views

Introduction

Addiction is any thinking or behavior that is habitual, repetitious and almost impossible or difficult to ignore regardless of the consequences. Many people have stayed in denial of addiction for a long time maybe because they were not enlightened enough about what addiction is. Most people believe that people can only be addicted to substance like drugs and alcohol. When they see someone not able to control the way they drink, they quickly pick that this person is addicted. ZUPCO. ”

To my understanding, substance abuse is better off because it is easily noticed and it can be treated as such. The worse addiction is the one that is done in privacy and in ignorance for example sex, masturbation, pornography nobody can notice that someone is coming from doing all these things of which someone can stay affected by it for a very long time and can cause more damage to mental, emotional, physiological and social wellbeing of that whole person. We have so many people who don’t consider themselves as addicted in this common equation but we have people who cause more harm to others through their addictions “regardless of the consequences”. We have people who are addicted to gossip, social media, attention seeking, always right attitude, prayer, scripture reading. In most cases we find these kinds of addictions in the church and sometimes with church leaders. Such cannot be corrected because they are at the helm of the organization so people are afraid to correct them. In most cases congregants react by leaving the church or backsliding. These addictions are very dangerous for Christians and our society at large. Sometimes you find people saying they cannot go back to church again because the church leaders did something that led them to leave church.

The major question is; how can someone get out of addiction? Well it is a journey to get out of addiction. The journey of getting out of addiction begins with a simple step of admitting powerlessness (2 Cor 4:7-10). Yes admitting that one is powerless over their life circumstances and that they have failed to manage their lives prepares for the journey to recovery. When someone admits that they are powerless over what they do and what life gives, it prepares that person to develop willingness to change. People who fail to recover from addictions it is because they did not admit powerlessness. Through the practical journey of helping the addicted, we have met people who bring their children who are drug addicts to get help and just because it is the parent who has developed the willingness for the child to change it did not work unless and until it is the child who decides to seek help. That is why in most cases we help the affected and the infected because it is the responsibility of the affected to help the infected develop the willingness to change through showing them love. I always say accommodation is not shelter; accommodation is a place inside the heart to belong. Every addicted person can find shelter anywhere and never come back home. What they seek is accommodation in the heart of which in most cases if its substance addiction they get it anywhere in the streets and they are so comfortable leaving the spacious home and stay in the streets where they are accommodated.

The second step to recovery is believing that a power greater than yourself could restore you to sanity (Rom 1:18-20). It is impossible to ever think one could attain recovery without the involvement of God the creator because one ought to report to the one who made him. Even in computer technology if a computer fails to run a certain function, there pops a page that asks the operator to give a report so that the developers can improve on the next version of software update. It is the same for people they out to submit to the life giver because at the end of the day what leads to addictions are the things people cannot control and don’t have answers to, except God. Third step to recovery is making a decision to turn one’s life over to the care of God as one begins to understand him (Psalms 61:1-8). The thought of turning our wills and our lives over can be attractive. When we give into our dependencies and compulsions, aren’t we giving control over to another power? Aren’t we in some way giving up personal responsibility for our lives? When we are overwhelmed and want to escape, our addictions can make us feel strong, safe, attractive, powerful and happy. So, in a sense we are very comfortable with the thought of giving up control of our wills and our lives. We can take a step to change our focus and turn our lives over to God instead of reverting to hiding places of the past (addictions). Fourth step is to make a search and fearless moral inventory of self (Neh 9:1-3). Most of us falter at the prospect of making an honest personal inventory. Rationalization and excuses for avoiding this step abound. The bottom line is that we know there is an anonymous amount of sadness awaiting us, and we fear the pain that facing the sadness will bring. The Jewish exiles who returned to Jerusalem after captivity in Babylon had lost touch with God. During the exile, they hadn’t been taught his laws, so naturally they hadn’t practiced them either. After rebuilding the city wall and the temple, the priests gathered the people together to read the book of the law. The people were overwhelmed with grief and began weeping, because their lives in no way measured up. Fifth step is admitting to yourself, life giver and another person the exact nature of wrongs (Acts 26: 12-18). As we work our recovery program, we go through a process of accepting the truth about our lives and the consequences of our choices. We may feel that we have to earn forgiveness instead of just receiving it. We may find it easier to forgive others who have hurt us than to forgive ourselves for the hurts we have caused.

Conclusion

Ready for God to remove all these character defects (Psalms 51:16–19). If we have sincerely practiced the previous steps we have probably found enough pain inside ourselves to break our hearts, facing the fact that brokenness is part of the human condition can be crushing. But if we have arrived at this point, it is probably a sign that we are ready for God to change us. Remember it is a journey of progress and not perfection. It is one day at a time without any hurry or much push from anyone. I will share with us the other six steps to complete the 12 steps in the next edition. Watch this space!
